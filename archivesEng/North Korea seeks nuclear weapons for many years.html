North Korea seeks years of nuclear weapons for global attention.

October 12, 2006 08:00.
Share.
  •  
  •  
  •  
  •  
  •  

North Korea's claim monday that it conducted a nuclear test has prompted Northeast Asian countries to think about how to deal with the resulting regional security problems. But North Korea's nuclear test is not surprising. Let us now look back at North Korea's decades-long history of pursuing nuclear weapons and the events that preceded the current crisis.

:: The hope of peace six years ago

Six years ago, South Korean President Kim Dae-jung declared that South Koreans did not have to live under the constant threat of war. His historic meeting with North Korean leader Kim Jong Il in 2000 raised hopes for peace and the eventual reunification of the divided North and South Korea since 1945.

Relations between the United States and North Korea have since improved. U.S. Secretary of State Madeleine Albright visited Pyongyang in late 2000 and met with Kim Jong Il.

:: The U.S. and North Korea were almost at war at one point.

Six years before that meeting, the United States and North Korea had been on the brink of war because of North Korea's nuclear weapons program.

Pyongyang has been trying to develop nuclear weapons since the 1950s. The United States and North Korea signed a framework agreement in 1994. The agreement provides for North Korea to freeze its weapons program in exchange for energy and other aid.

:: Preparations began in the late 1990s

On Monday, the North Korean government announced a nuclear test, breaking its commitment to the United States and the world.

Many U.S. weapons experts and government officials say North Korea may have been preparing for a nuclear test in the late 1990s.

:: Bush: Don't believe in Pyongyang

In 2001, Bush became president of the United States. The Bush administration has decided to re-evaluate its relations with Pyongyang and has expressed doubts about whether it can confirm North Korea's compliance with the framework agreement.

Later, President Bush made it clear that he did not believe in Pyongyang.
"The North Korean regime has missiles and weapons of mass destruction while starving people," he
said. Such States, as well as their terrorist allies, constitute an axis of evil and a threat to world peace. "

:: Secret enrichment of uranium

In October 2002, Kelly, a senior U.S. diplomat, revealed that North Korea was secretly enriching uranium. Enriched uranium is the fuel for nuclear weapons.

"I told the North Koreans that they must immediately and unequivocally cancel this clandestine nuclear weapons program," Kelly said. "

North Korea responded by restarting sealed facilities, reprocessing nuclear fuel rods, expelling U.N. weapons inspectors and withdrawing from the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty.

The United States and other countries immediately stopped energy and other aid to North Korea.

:: U.S. Tough South Korea Stays In Contact

The U.S. policy of relatively tough on North Korea contrasts sharply with its ally South Korea's policy of maintaining contact with North Korea.

South Korea is providing aid to North Korea and investing in it in the hope of guiding the North to correct its behavior.

Some political analysts believe the South Korean government's efforts have paid little for it.

"South Korea should adopt a stronger policy to deal with the possibility that North Korea could develop a nuclear weapon, " said Quan Hong-yin, a professor of political science at The National University of Busan. The South Korean government has been too accommodating. "

:: North Korea changes its attitude frequently

The United States, China, South Korea, Japan and Russia began negotiations with North Korea in 2003 to de-escalate tensions on the Korean peninsula.

As demonstrated in other crises, the North Korean authorities, during the negotiations, have shown reconciliation and provocations.

North Korea first said it had built a nuclear weapon. Then, a year ago, he agreed in principle to abandon the nuclear weapons program in exchange for economic and security benefits.

:: Exit talks to protest U.S. sanctions

Pyongyang later refused to continue the talks in a show of anger at the United States, which accused North Korea of illegal money laundering and the manufacture of counterfeit U.S. dollar bills and imposed sanctions on the North.

A week before Pyongyang said it would conduct a nuclear test, North Korea's deputy foreign minister, Choe Seion, told the United Nations General Assembly that U.S. financial sanctions against North Korea were unjustified. Under such circumstances, he said, the North Korean government cannot return to negotiations.

"This is a matter of principle," Cui said. We can't give in. "

:: Hill: Will not recognize North Korea as a nuclear state

In past crises, North Korea has frequently threatened to get other countries to accept its demands.

But on Monday, Hill, the chief U.S. representative to the six-party talks, said Washington would not follow North Korea's risky game.

"I think North Korea's intention is that if they conduct a nuclear test, we might be angry, but in a while we will accept the fact that North Korea is a nuclear-weapon state," Hill said. We will not recognize North Korea as a nuclear state. North Korea must give up its nuclear weapons. "

North Korea doesn't plan to give up its nuclear program? *

Some weapons control experts say North Korea may never have intended to give up its nuclear weapons program. The closed-door state feels it must possess nuclear weapons from the guard, or threaten other countries.

They say it is increasingly possible for the United Nations to impose sanctions on North Korea, but that does not force the North Korean leader to give up its nuclear weapons. After all, if Monday's test turns out to be a nuclear test, it could be said that North Korea has fulfilled its 50-year-old desire to become a nuclear state.
