North Vietnam launched the "Spring Offensive" at the end of January 1968, in the face of u.S. and South Vietnamese superior firepower, the Southern Liberation Front suffered heavy casualties and lost all control territory, but the "Spring Offensive" is still considered by many analysts to be a strategic victory in Hanoi.

Harish Mehta, a historian who studies Indochina, argues that the "New Year offensive" was a military defeat for Hanoi, but the full-scale offensive promoted the U.S. anti-war movement and dealt a psychological blow to the U.S. government. He believes the "spring offensive" has a direct impact on President Johnson's decision-making. Two months after the NATO offensive, a thriving U.S. anti-war movement has put Johnson's Vietnam policy in jeopardy, and Johnson says he will not seek re-election.

The "spring offensive" exposed the failure of Johnson and U.S. commander William Westmoreland in Vietnam to deliver on their previous promises. Before the "spring offensive" they had been saying that the U.S. strategy in Vietnam was working and that "the dawn is before". Mehta said the "spring offensive" allowed the United States to abandon its intention to win the war and begin seeking a breakthrough in negotiations.

"War crimes in Vietnam"

At that time, more than half of the American population opposed the escalation of the American war in Vietnam. Anti-war protests in the United States intensified after the "Spring Offensive" and in April 1968 anti-war activists occupied Columbia University, prompting a strong police expulsion.

In addition to fierce anti-war protests, the "Spring Offensive" promoted civil diplomacy between North Vietnam and the American public. At that time, Chomsky and many other famous American scholars were invited to visit Vietnam to oppose the continuation of the war.

"What our government is doing is making death, killing each other is their business," said George Wald, a Harvard professor and Nobel Prize-winning professor at the University of Science and Technology, in a 1969 speech at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology before visiting North Vietnam. "

Red Quotes and the Black Panther Party.

During the Vietnam War, when the organized anti-war movement in the United States intensified, China began the Cultural Revolution. The anti-culture, anti-trend and anti-institutional expressions and activities of the American anti-war movement, as well as the student movement, echoed the "revolt" in the Chinese Cultural Revolution.

Martin Luther King, an African-American pastor and leader of the civil rights movement, has been speaking out since 1967, saying that the United States wanted to enslave Vietnam as its own colony. His anti-war stance cost him the support of President Johnson and some white scantilyths.

Three months after the Start of the U.S. New Year offensive began in April 1968, Vietnam agreed to negotiate with the United States, announcing the assassination of Martin Luther King, the day after. Nguyen Lien-kyu, author of "The War in Hanoi," said Chinese leaders told Vietnamese Prime Minister Fan Wentong, who visited Beijing in April that Vietnam had agreed to negotiate too much concessions to the U.S., and that the assassination of Martin Luther King Jr. and North Vietnam's announcement of the talks might be linked.

In April of the same year, Mao Zedong issued a statement in support of the fight against violence in the United States. In 1963, Mao Zedong, at Robert William's request, also issued a "statement in support of the just struggle of black Americans against the racial discrimination of American imperialism."

After the cultural revolution in China began, Thei Newton, founder of the black left-wing radical party Black Panther Party, also asked members of the Black Panther Party to learn "Red Quotes" (Chairman Mao's Quotes) and learn about the Communist Party's "Service to the People" and "The Mass Line". They began to provide free breakfast for poor children in the black community, to politically inspire the community, and to give the black community a sense of community. The Black Panther Party became a black "class liberation" organization.

At the time, Hollywood star Jane Fonda also expressed support for the Black Lives Matter movement and the Black Panther Party, calling it "our revolutionary pioneer... We must support them with love, money, propaganda and adventure." "Revolution is the action of love; we are the descendants of revolution, born rebellious, born with blood," she said in the early 1970s.

Shoi Newton received an invitation to visit China in 1970. When he learned that China-U.S. relations had eased, and president Nixon was scheduled to visit China in 1972, he decided to go to China before Nixon. In September 1971, Shoi Newton came to China. He had hoped to be received by Mao Zedong, but he was not able to do so, but he still received high courtesy in China.

During his 10 days in China, Shoi Newton met with Zhou Enlai twice, including one with Jiang Qing, Mao's wife. When he arrived at the airport in many of his visits to China, he was greeted by a large crowd of people who held up their "red quotes". Chinese welcomers chanted slogans such as "Support the Black Panther Party, Bring down U.S. Imperialism" and "Support the American people, bring down Nixon's U.S. imperialist regime."

During his stay in China, the Chinese government arranged for Shoi Newton to meet with representatives of the South Vietnam National Liberation Front Party, meet and dine with the Ambassadors of North Korea and Tanzania.

Newton said China is "a free and liberated place under socialist government." Some say that during the visit, China offered to offer political asylum to Thei Newton, who is embroiled in a lawsuit in the United States.

"Born in the United States"

In 1981, American singer Bruce Springsteen wrote the song "Born to America" (Born to the United States), which was a huge success and was listed as one of the greatest songs of all time. But interestingly, the song is now often played as a quasi-national anthem that expresses American patriotism.

The songwriters initially used the song to tell the story of the suffering of excluded Vietnam veterans and their protests against mainstream American society, with lyrics to the effect that those veterans were excluded from the American dream after serving in the Vietnam War.

Springsteen's song mentions veterans who fought in the famous Battle of the Creek Seok during the Vietnam War. The Battle of Xishan was the precursor to the spring offensive and the worst u.S. military battle ever fought during the Vietnam War, when U.S. commander in Vietnam, Westmoreland, even considered mobilizing tactical nuclear weapons against North Vietnamese troops attacking The U.S. Marines.

The song, intended to express anti-war content, has been considered a song for more than 30 years that represents all Americans, becoming a party campaign rally in the United States, and a must-play song for the American spirit at sporting events. Republican President Ronald Reagan used it as a campaign song in the 1980s. Reagan said the song expressed the American dream and sang the voice of millions of Americans.

By January 20, 2009, when President Barack Obama took office, he referred to the Battle of The Brook and Mountains in his inaugural address. In his memory of the historical pioneers of American love, Obama referred to the pioneers " who fought to the death for us, in Concord, Gettysburg, Normandy, and The Hill." "

The spring offensive 50 years ago promoted the fierce anti-war movement in the United States, but the time of transit, and the song "Born in america" was misunderstood and tasted, the United States anti-war radicalism and ideas have become too much of a cloud. Supporters of the U.S. government have turned their backs on the original activist protesters, who praised the left's authoritarianism and rejected democracy when denouncing right-wing centralization. 50 years ago, the Vietnam New Year offensive: The United States was radicalized against war.
