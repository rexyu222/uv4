Until the eve of the collapse of the Soviet Union, the issue of the Great Famine was long banned from being raised and discussed as a no-go zone.

          Declassified the archives, carefully arranged to keep visitors out of the famine.

          Ukraine marks the 85th anniversary of the Great Famine on Saturday. For this purpose, the archives of Ukraine's National Security Service have released a batch of declassified files that tell the story of the famine known to foreigners in Ukraine at the time and how the Soviet regime covered up the famine by various means.

          During the famine of the 1930s, he visited Ukraine, where he was a former French prime minister and leader of the radical Socialist Party. The Soviet authorities carefully and carefully arranged Mr. Heriot's travel routes, not allowing him to see the famine-stricken Ukrainian countryside, but instead allowing the famous French politician to visit some of the model collective farms.

          The strategy worked, French politicians defended the Soviet Union.

          The Soviet authorities also banned the wearandiating, unsightly-looking hungry peasants approaching Heriot," while also keeping French expatriates settled in Ukraine out of him.

          The efforts of the Soviet authorities have paid off. On his return to France, Heriot's defended the Soviet Union against news of the Famine in the Western world at the time, saying he had seen no evidence of a famine in the Soviet Union.

          Japanese publicity feels the contrast between factory high-rises and thin farmers

          A whistleblower report described the impression of a Japanese pro-president traveling in Ukraine. The Japanese diplomat said Ukrainian farmers were more shabby and thin and tired than Soviet farmers in other regions, a sharp contrast to the large factories and high-rise buildings being built in The city of Donbass, Ukraine, and residential dormitories.

          German comme, famine took place in food-producing areas where most villagers starved to death.

          The German Consulate General and diplomats in Ukraine were the focus of surveillance by the Soviet secret police. But Germany has the most detailed information and knowledge of the Great Famine in Ukraine. The secret police report referred to discussions between the German and Polish and Japanese diplomats in Ukraine about the famine.

          In a report sent back to the Foreign Ministry in Berlin in 1933, the Intercepted German Comme noted that the great famine had swept through Ukraine, and that the main feature was that all the famines had occurred in the food-producing areas known as the Black Land. Eighty percent of the villagers in rural Ukraine have starved to death.

          The authorities tried to cover up the news of the famine, which still pierced the Iron Curtain.

          Much of the release of the files came from reports written by undercover agents at the secret police consulates in foreign countries and other foreign agencies in Ukraine, as well as other tip-off materials, as well as surveillance and analysis of Western diplomats and other foreigners operating in Ukraine during the Great Famine, said Kogut, head of the archives of Ukraine's National Security Agency.

          Kogut said the declassified files, released, showed that although the Soviet authorities tried to cover up the famine, the news of the great famine could still pass through the Iron Curtain for the international community to know.

          The Soviets called the famine report a lie, and the Vatican knew the truth.

          At that time, the Soviet authorities had used some Western reports of the Great Famine as false hoodies made up by foreign agents. After the October Revolution, the Soviet State gate was gradually closed to death. By the 1930s, just as it was hard for the world today to understand what was happening inside North Korea, the outside world could not understand what was happening in the Soviet Union.

          But the Western world can still learn the truth through many channels. In addition to the diplomats' reports, the Vatican also learned about the Great Famine through Catholics in Ukraine.

          Austrian bishop raises first-hand picture of engineer

          The Ukrainian news agency said cardinal Innicha, then the Cardinal of Vienna, may have been the first prominent figure in the world to make a public appeal to help Ukrainian farmers during the Great Famine. Innicha called on the international community to provide assistance to Ukraine and organized fund-raising activities, and he convened relevant international seminars in Vienna in particular.

          Innicha's activities were supported by Austrian chemical engineer Wenigberg. Mr. Veneberg, who was working at a factory on the outskirts of Moscow, has made several trips to the Kharkiv region, an industrial town in eastern Ukraine, because of his working relationship. Wenigberg took many first-hand photographs of the Great Famine, taken out of the Soviet Union by Austrian diplomats, as evidence of the Famine, to support Bishop Innicha's fund-raising efforts.

          The Soviet Union banned the discussion of the Great Famine.

          Ukrainian media say that during the Soviet era, the Great Famine was used as a no-go zone in Ukraine, and the authorities banned people from bringing up and discussing the topic. It was not until the late 1980s that the study of the Great Famine began in the Soviet Union, and some relevant information began to be published in public. But in recent years, some of the world's most important books on the study of the Great Famine in Ukraine have again been added to the list of so-called extremist publications by the Russian authorities.

          Mr. Ikhlov, a current affairs commentator, said he had been under the impression that the Soviet authorities had been trying to make people forget about the great famine.

          Ikhlov: "The issue of famine is not as riskful as it is involving state secrets, but the issue around it is silent in the former Soviet Union." In my memory, there may be only one literary work disguised as a reference to the Great Famine, written by a peasant writer. There is also a work about the famine that erupted two years after the end of World War II. Perhaps neither of the authors is a well-known writer, and the authorities have little influence before the authorities allow their work to be published. "

          Let the world know the truth, many countries condemn the crimes of communism.

          Ukraine is currently pushing for more countries around the world to recognize the Great Famine as an act of national extermination against the Ukrainians. On the occasion of the 85th anniversary of the Great Famine in Ukraine, the Polish Senate passed a resolution condemning the communist regime while remembering the victims of the famine. Polish senators say more people around the world should be made aware of the tragedy of the famine and the nature of communism.

          Slovakia's President Kiska, who has just visited Ukraine, said in Kiev on Friday that the famine in Ukraine was a serious crime, with the number of people starving to death even higher than the number of people in Slovakia today.
