Moscow -

The secret police were a key supporting force for the communist system of the former Soviet Union. Thirty years after the collapse of communism in Eastern Europe, most countries have turned the former secret police headquarters into a museum and a tourist attraction. There are also States that continue to use the former secret police as the site of the new intelligence agency. Unlike others, under Mr Putin, the secret police are stronger in Russia today than they were in the past.

Turn ingathy museum into a tourist attraction

Deutsche Welle recently reported that a new walking tour has begun in eastern Berlin to introduce the KGB, the soviet secret police. The tourist service is doing well, with visitors wandering through the converted garden streets to learn about the Activities of the Cold War-era KGB, the report said.

Towards the end of World War II, the Soviet Red Army first occupied the then unspoiled building, the headquarters of the former Nazi German Engineers in eastern Berlin, where german residents were ordered to leave, and the area later became the Soviet headquarters in East Germany. At the height of the Cold War in the 1960s, the KGB, the Soviet secret police, and another major intelligence agency, the General Military Intelligence Service of the Soviet Union, entered the area. After the collapse of the Soviet Union and the reunification of Germany, the two intelligence agencies, together with the Soviet/Russian army in Germany, withdrew in the early 1990s. The SITE OF THE KGB IS NOW A MUSEUM, AND THE AREA HAS BEEN TRANSFORMED INTO RESIDENTIAL AREAS BY DEVELOPERS.

The former Communist regime in East Germany also had its own secret police agency, Stasi. On the night of the fall of the Berlin Wall, large numbers of demonstrators stormed the Stasi headquarters in eastern Berlin. Stacy's headquarters later became the Stasi Museum.

The Czechoslovak Communist Intelligence Agency (STB) was formed after World War II under the help of the KGB. The secret police agency is not as famous as Stacy and the KGB, but it is known for its expertise in collecting and stealing Western industrial and technological intelligence. Especially in the 1970s, the role of Czechoslovak intelligence became very important as the gap between the Soviet socialist camp and the West widened in economic and technological fields.

The secret police agency was disbanded after the fall of the Communist Party, and the old city centre street, where the headquarters is located, is now a tourist attraction in Prague.

But a book published five years ago about the former Communist Secret Police revealed that the Czechoslovak secret police had dozens of other secret offices in Prague that year, none of which had an official address. The secret police are also responsible for spying on foreigners and dissidents. Mao Zedong's followers, like Ukrainian nationalists, were under surveillance at the time.

Introduction to the history of bloody persecution

The basement of the headquarters of the secret police headquarters of the Hungarian communist regime was also used as torture and prisons. The headquarters building of the secret police in Budapest was bought in 2000 by the Public Foundation for Social Studies in Central and Eastern Europe and is now known as the "Museum of Terror", which showcases the bloody rule of the Communist era.

The former KGB headquarters in the three Baltic states are now turned into museums. The former KGB headquarters building in Estonia, located in the heart of the capital Tallinn's Old Town, is now an upscale apartment residence. The KGB Museum is located in the basement of the building and displays exhibits about political persecution and bloody repression. In addition, a new Soviet occupation museum has been built in Tallinn, the capital of Estonia.

After the former KGB headquarters in Latvia became the KGB Museum, the exhibits also reflect editing and persecution by the Soviet secret police. Ushakov, the former mayor of riga, said two years ago that during municipal construction, he had stumbled upon the building's secret underground passages. Another location in the centre of Riga also houses a Soviet occupation museum.

After the Nazi occupation of World War II, the KGB headquarters in Vilnius, the capital of Lithuania, became the headquarters of the Gestapo in Germany. After the war it was again turned into the headquarters of the KGB, which is now the Museum of Soviet and Nazi Occupation. Inside are cells, various physical and execution chambers, to introduce visitors to the brutality of the secret police.

The KGB headquarters in Almaty, Kazakhstan's former capital, was also turned into a museum in 2003, but closed two years later. The building was later acquired by a private company and was demolished last year with plans to build a hotel on the site. Some Kazakh activists staged protests over the cause last year. They say the construction of hotels was a way for people to forget about history during the Stalin era, when dozens of Kazakh intellectual elites were executed there.

The former secret police building is still in use.

In Communist-era Poland, the interior minister was also the head of the KGB, the secret police. During the Polish round table in 1989, the topic of opposition control of the Ministry of the Interior and the secret police was first raised. The Secret Police were disbanded immediately after the Unite union came to power. Poland currently has two main agencies, the National Internal Affairs Office and the Intelligence Service. The Polish Internal Affairs Department, which currently works closely with Western intelligence agencies, still uses the former Interior Ministry building in Warsaw.

Located in the center of Kiev, a few steps from Independence Square, Ukraine's National Security Agency also uses the former Ukrainian KGB headquarters building. During the Nazi occupation, it was also the gestanos headquarters. In 2016, a plaque was hung on a wall next to an entrance to the building in memory of the victims of political persecution. In his speech, the head of Ukraine's National Security Agency, Grychak, said that many people entered the gate by car during the purge, but few were able to get out. He called on people to remember the history of political persecution.

Russia only changes signboards, influences more than the KGB

In The Lubyanka Square in central Moscow, today's Russian National Security Agency is located in the former KGB headquarters in several buildings. Several other nearby buildings used to be the city of Moscow, the KGB of the Moscow Oblast, and now the Moscow City and the Moscow State Security Agency.

Political scholars believe that most of Russia's elite today comes from security intelligence agencies. The KGB was also controlled by the Party and the Communist Party of China (CPC) Central Committee in the past, but under Putin, the russian security apparatus today has more influence than the former KGB.

Historian Zubov argues that this particular background of Putin and others determines their worldview and view of events, and even the direction of the country.

Zubov: "I'm not talking about Russia becoming the Soviet Union again, but Russia is now in a different era of authoritarianism than the Soviet Union." There was a strong ideology in the Soviet era, but not now. "

Not only is Belarus' security apparatus still the NAME KGB, but its headquarters are also located in the former KGB building in the centre of the capital Minsk.
